# Veins of Metal: The Great Trade Networks of the Bronze Age

## Overview
The Bronze Age marked a pivotal transition in human history, shifting societies from the localized stone-tool economies of the Neolithic to complex, interdependent networks of exchange. The catalyst for this transformation was the discovery of alloying: combining copper with tin to create bronze, a metal far superior in strength and durability.

However, nature rarely placed copper and tin in the same geographic location. Copper was relatively common, found in the Alps, the Balkans, and the Urals, but tin was exceedingly rare, sourced from distant fringes like Cornwall in Britain, Brittany in France, or the mountains of Central Asia. This geological scarcity forced prehistoric peoples to establish vast, transcontinental trade routes. These "veins of metal" did more than transport ore; they acted as conduits for language, religious beliefs, artistic styles, and diplomatic customs, effectively shrinking the known world and laying the groundwork for the first great civilizations.

## Timeline of Trade Expansion

| Period | Approximate Dates | Key Development |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Early Bronze Age** | 3300 – 2000 BCE | Emergence of regional copper smelting; initial long-distance tin searches. |
| **Middle Bronze Age** | 2000 – 1500 BCE | Establishment of the "Amber Road" and formalized prestige goods exchange. |
| **Late Bronze Age** | 1500 – 1200 BCE | Peak of internationalism; palace economies (Mycenaean, Hittite, Egyptian) link via the Mediterranean. |
| **Bronze Age Collapse** | c. 1200 – 1150 BCE | Systemic failure of trade networks leading to a regional "Dark Age." |

## Key Facts

### The Logistics of Exchange
Trade in the prehistoric era was rarely a direct "merchant-to-consumer" transaction. Instead, it functioned through **down-the-line exchange**. A piece of tin from Cornwall might pass through a dozen different tribal intermediaries—each trading it for food, salt, or pottery—before finally reaching a smith in the Mediterranean.

### Prestige Goods and Power
Not everything traded was utilitarian. The elite of the Bronze Age traded in "prestige goods" to signal status. **Amber** from the Baltic coast was highly prized in the south, while **lapis lazuli** from Afghanistan traveled thousands of miles to adorn the jewelry of Egyptian pharaohs and Mesopotamian kings.

### The Mediterranean Nexus
By the Late Bronze Age, the Mediterranean Sea became a superhighway. The Uluburun shipwreck (off the coast of Turkey) provides a snapshot of this globalization, containing copper ingots from Cyprus, tin from Central Asia, ebony from Africa, and glass from Egypt, all on one vessel.

## Did You Know?

*   **The Tin Mystery:** For decades, historians debated where the tin for the Mediterranean civilizations came from. Isotopic analysis has since proven that some tin traveled from as far as Cornwall, England, to the Levant.
*   **The First "Currency":** While coins didn't exist, copper and tin were often cast into standardized shapes, such as "oxhide ingots," which served as a primitive form of currency due to their predictable weight.
*   **Cultural Contagion:** Trade networks were the primary way that the chariot—a revolutionary military technology—spread from the Eurasian steppes to the heart of Egypt and Greece.
