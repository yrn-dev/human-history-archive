# Echoes in Stone: The Megalithic Cultures of Prehistory

## Overview
Across the landscapes of Western Europe and beyond, the prehistoric world left behind an enduring mystery: the megaliths. Derived from the Greek words *megas* (great) and *lithos* (stone), these monuments represent some of the most ambitious engineering feats of the Neolithic and Early Bronze Age. While Stonehenge remains the most iconic, it is part of a global phenomenon of stone-building that reflects a profound shift in human social organization.

These structures were not merely piles of rock; they were sophisticated expressions of cosmology, territoriality, and communal identity. Whether they served as astronomical calendars, ancestral shrines, or markers of political power, megaliths indicate that prehistoric societies possessed complex hierarchies and a deep understanding of geometry and celestial movements. From the towering menhirs of Brittany to the intricate passage tombs of Ireland, these monuments transformed the natural landscape into a sacred geography.

## Key Facts

### Engineering and Labor
The construction of sites like Stonehenge required an immense mobilization of labor. The larger sarsen stones weigh up to 30 tons, while the smaller bluestones were transported from the Preseli Hills in Wales—over 140 miles away. This suggests a highly organized society capable of feeding and directing hundreds of workers over several generations.

### Astronomical Alignment
Megaliths were frequently aligned with solar and lunar events. At Stonehenge, the primary axis aligns with the summer solstice sunrise and winter solstice sunset. Similarly, the passage tomb at Newgrange is engineered so that a beam of light illuminates the inner chamber only during the winter solstice, suggesting that these cultures viewed their dead as being linked to the cycles of the sun.

### Diverse Forms
Megalithic architecture generally falls into three categories:
*   **Menhirs:** Single standing stones, often serving as markers.
*   **Dolmens:** Portal-like structures (a large capstone supported by uprights), typically serving as tombs.
*   **Cromlechs:** Circular arrangements of stones, often used for communal rituals.

## Did You Know?

*   **Acoustic Engineering:** Recent archaeological studies suggest that the arrangement of stones in some circles may have been designed to amplify sound or create specific acoustic echoes during rituals.
*   **Not Just Europe:** While famous in Europe, megalithic cultures existed globally, including in Korea (where some of the world's largest dolmens are found) and parts of Africa and India.
*   **The "Moving" Stones:** To move the massive blocks, prehistoric builders likely used a combination of wooden rollers, sledges, and grease, pulling the stones with ropes made from animal hide or plant fibers.
