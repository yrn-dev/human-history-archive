# The Great Depression: The Decade of Economic Despair

## Overview
The Great Depression was the most severe economic downturn in the history of the industrialized Western world, lasting roughly from 1929 to 1939. While often associated solely with the dramatic "crash" of the stock market, the Depression was actually the result of a systemic failure involving agricultural overproduction, unsustainable consumer debt, and a fragile international banking system.

The crisis began in the United States but rapidly globalized due to the interconnected nature of post-WWI loans and trade. As banks failed and currencies collapsed, unemployment skyrocketed—reaching nearly 25% in the U.S.—and millions of families lost their homes and life savings. The era was defined by "Hoovervilles" (shanty towns) and the ecological catastrophe of the Dust Bowl, which devastated the American Midwest. Recovery only arrived through a combination of Franklin D. Roosevelt’s "New Deal" interventions and the massive industrial mobilization required for World War II.

## Key Facts

*   **Bank Failures:** In the early 1930s, thousands of banks collapsed because they had invested depositors' money in the stock market. Since deposits were not insured at the time, millions of people lost their entire life savings overnight.
*   **The Dust Bowl:** A combination of severe drought and poor farming practices led to massive dust storms in the Great Plains, forcing hundreds of thousands of "Okies" to migrate west to California in search of work.
*   **The New Deal:** President Franklin D. Roosevelt shifted the role of the federal government from a passive observer to an active manager of the economy, creating agencies like the WPA (Works Progress Administration) and the CCC (Civilian Conservation Corps).
*   **Deflationary Spiral:** Prices for goods dropped sharply, which sounds positive but actually crippled the economy; businesses earned less, leading to wage cuts and further reductions in consumer spending.

## Did You Know?

1. **The "Forgotten" Hunger:** During the height of the crisis, "breadlines" and "soup kitchens" became permanent fixtures of urban landscapes, as charities were the only source of food for millions.
2. **Gold Standard Constraints:** Many historians argue that the crisis lasted so long because countries clung to the Gold Standard, which prevented governments from expanding their money supply to stimulate the economy.
3. **Psychological Scarring:** The Depression created a "scarcity mindset" for an entire generation (the "Depression Generation"), characterized by extreme frugality and a lifelong distrust of stock market speculation.
