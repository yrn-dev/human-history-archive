# The Great Depression: The Decade of Economic Despair

## Overview
The Great Depression was the most severe economic downturn in the history of the industrialized Western world, lasting roughly from 1929 to 1939. While often associated solely with the dramatic "crash" of the stock market, the Depression was actually the result of a complex convergence of systemic weaknesses: overproduction in agriculture and industry, unsustainable consumer debt, and a fragile international banking system.

The crisis transformed the relationship between citizens and their governments. In the United States, the era saw the transition from *laissez-faire* capitalism to the "New Deal" era, where the federal government assumed responsibility for the social welfare of its citizens. Globally, the economic misery provided a fertile breeding ground for political extremism, contributing directly to the rise of totalitarian regimes in Europe and Asia, which eventually culminated in World War II.

## Key Facts

### The Banking Crisis
The collapse was exacerbated by "bank runs," where panicked depositors rushed to withdraw their savings. Because banks held only a fraction of deposits in reserve, thousands of institutions failed, wiping out the life savings of millions of families.

### The Dust Bowl
Environmental catastrophe compounded economic ruin. A combination of severe drought and poor farming practices turned the American Great Plains into a wasteland of dust storms. This forced a mass migration of "Okies" and other displaced farmers toward California.

### The New Deal
President Franklin D. Roosevelt implemented a series of programs categorized by the "Three Rs": **Relief** (for the unemployed), **Recovery** (of the economy), and **Reform** (of the financial system to prevent future crashes). This included the creation of the FDIC to insure bank deposits.

## Did You Know?

* **The "Hoovervilles":** Shanty towns built by homeless people were named "Hoovervilles" after President Herbert Hoover, whom the public blamed for the government's perceived inaction.
* **Deflationary Spiral:** During the Depression, prices didn't just stop rising—they plummeted. This meant that while things were "cheaper," businesses couldn't make a profit, leading to more layoffs and further drops in demand.
* **The Gold Standard:** Many historians argue that the crisis lasted so long because nations clung to the Gold Standard, which prevented governments from expanding the money supply to stimulate their economies.
