# The Great Depression: The Decade of Economic Despair

## Overview
The Great Depression was the most severe economic downturn in the history of the industrialized modern world, lasting roughly from 1929 to 1939. While often associated with the dramatic imagery of "bread lines" and "Hoovervilles," the crisis was a complex systemic failure triggered by a combination of stock market speculation, banking fragility, and a catastrophic drop in consumer demand.

The collapse began in the United States but rapidly evolved into a global phenomenon due to the interconnected nature of international trade and war debts from World War I. As banks failed and currencies collapsed, unemployment soared to nearly 25% in the U.S. and even higher in some European nations. The era was defined by a fundamental shift in the role of government; the crisis ended the *laissez-faire* approach to economics, leading to the birth of the modern welfare state and increased federal intervention in the economy through initiatives like Franklin D. Roosevelt’s "New Deal."

## Timeline of Collapse and Recovery

| Year | Event | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1929** | Black Tuesday (Oct 29) | The stock market crash triggers a financial panic. |
| **1930** | Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act | Increased tariffs stifle international trade, deepening the crisis. |
| **1932** | Peak Unemployment | Global economic output hits its lowest point; mass poverty peaks. |
| **1933** | The New Deal Begins | FDR launches aggressive federal programs to provide relief and reform. |
| **1935** | Social Security Act | Establishes a permanent safety net for the elderly and disabled. |
| **1939** | Outbreak of WWII | Massive military spending finally ends the depression. |

## Key Facts
*   **The Banking Crisis:** Between 1929 and 1933, thousands of banks failed because they had invested depositors' money in the stock market. When panicked citizens rushed to withdraw their savings (known as "bank runs"), the banks lacked the liquidity to pay, wiping out the life savings of millions.
*   **The Dust Bowl:** The economic collapse was compounded by an environmental disaster in the Great Plains. Severe drought and poor farming practices led to massive dust storms, forcing thousands of "Okies" and other displaced farmers to migrate west toward California.
*   **Deflationary Spiral:** Prices for goods and services plummeted. While lower prices seem beneficial, this caused businesses to earn less, leading to wage cuts and further layoffs, which in turn lowered demand even more.
*   **The New Deal:** President Roosevelt introduced the "Three Rs": **Relief** for the unemployed, **Recovery** of the economy, and **Reform** of the financial system to prevent a future crash (including the creation of the FDIC).

## Did You Know?
1. **The "Forgotten" Recovery:** While the New Deal provided essential relief, most historians agree that the economy did not fully recover until the massive industrial mobilization for World War II.
2. **Gold Standard Rigidity:** Many economists argue that the crisis lasted longer because countries clung to the Gold Standard, which prevented governments from expanding their money supply to stimulate growth.
3. **Psychological Scars:** The Depression created a "scarcity mindset" for an entire generation, leading many survivors to hoard food and money for the rest of their lives.
