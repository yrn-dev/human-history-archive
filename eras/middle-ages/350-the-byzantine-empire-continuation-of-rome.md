# The Eternal Empire: Byzantium as the Continuation of Rome

## Overview
The Byzantine Empire is often discussed as a separate entity from the Roman Empire, yet to its own citizens, there was no such thing as "Byzantine." They called themselves *Rhōmaioi* (Romans) until the very fall of Constantinople in 1453. Historically, the Byzantine Empire was the direct continuation of the Roman state in the East, surviving for nearly a millennium after the collapse of the Western Roman Empire in 476 AD.

The transition was not a sudden break but a gradual evolution. As the Western provinces succumbed to Germanic migrations and political instability, the center of gravity shifted to Constantinople—the "New Rome"—founded by Constantine the Great. This eastern half preserved Roman law, administration, and political structures, while blending them with Greek culture and Orthodox Christianity. While the West entered the Early Middle Ages, the East flourished as a beacon of antiquity, acting as a shield for Europe against various eastern incursions and preserving the classical knowledge that would eventually spark the Renaissance.

## Key Facts
*   **The Justinian Code:** Emperor Justinian I oversaw the *Corpus Juris Civilis*, a massive synthesis of Roman law. This work became the foundation for many modern legal systems in Europe and Latin America.
*   **The Strategic Capital:** Constantinople was geographically unmatched, situated on the Bosphorus strait. Its massive "Theodosian Walls" made it the most fortified city in the world for centuries.
*   **Cultural Synthesis:** The empire functioned as a bridge. While the administration remained Roman and the religion was Christian, the lingua franca shifted from Latin to Greek by the 7th century.
*   **The Varangian Guard:** The emperors employed an elite bodyguard composed of Norsemen and Anglo-Saxons, illustrating the empire's vast diplomatic and mercenary networks.

## Did You Know?
1. **Greek Fire:** The Byzantines possessed a terrifying secret weapon called "Greek Fire," a liquid incendiary that could burn on water. The exact formula remains a mystery to modern chemists.
2. **Preserving the Classics:** While much of the works of Plato and Aristotle were lost in the West, Byzantine scholars meticulously copied and preserved these texts, ensuring their survival.
3. **The Hagia Sophia:** For nearly a thousand years, the Hagia Sophia was the largest cathedral in the world, featuring a dome that seemed to "float" due to innovative architectural engineering.
