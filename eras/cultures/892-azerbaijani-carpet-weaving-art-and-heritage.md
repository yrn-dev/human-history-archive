# Azerbaijani Carpet Weaving: A Tapestry of Art and Heritage

Azerbaijani carpet weaving is not merely a craft; it is a profound visual language that has encapsulated the history, spirituality, and social fabric of the South Caucasus for millennia. Recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, these textiles serve as a bridge between the nomadic traditions of the steppes and the sophisticated urban artistry of the Silk Road.

### Overview
The art of Azerbaijani carpets is defined by its extraordinary diversity. Rather than a single style, the tradition is divided into several distinct regional schools—most notably Guba, Shirvan, Baku, Ganja, and Karabakh. Each school is characterized by specific geometric patterns, color palettes, and weaving techniques. While the Guba and Shirvan carpets are often noted for their intricate, small-scale patterns and precision, the Karabakh carpets are celebrated for their large-scale floral compositions and vibrant hues.

At its core, the Azerbaijani carpet is a symbolic map. The motifs used—ranging from stylized animals and mythical creatures to complex floral arrangements—often reflect the weaver's environment, beliefs, and emotional state. The use of natural dyes derived from pomegranate skins, madder root, and indigo ensures that these textiles maintain a luminosity that lasts for centuries.

### Key Facts
*   **The Symbolic Palette:** Red is the most dominant color, symbolizing fire, energy, and life, while blue represents the sky and peace.
*   **Regional Specialization:** The *Karabakh* school is famous for the "Chelebi" and "Malibeyli" patterns, which often mirror the lush gardens of the region.
*   **The Loom as a Tool of Storytelling:** Historically, carpets were woven by women, serving as a medium to pass down familial legends and cultural identity from mother to daughter.
*   **Materiality:** Traditionally crafted from high-quality wool, though silk was frequently used for luxury pieces intended for royalty or religious sites.

### Did You Know?
1. **The "Buta" Motif:** The almond-shaped *Buta* pattern, ubiquitous in Azerbaijani carpets, is a symbol of fire and eternity, reflecting the ancient Zoroastrian roots of the region.
2. **A Global Reach:** During the Middle Ages, Azerbaijani carpets were so highly prized that they were frequently listed as diplomatic gifts between the Caliphates and the Byzantine Empire.
3. **Mathematical Precision:** Despite being hand-woven without written blueprints, the complex symmetry of these carpets relies on an intuitive, sophisticated understanding of mathematics and geometry.
