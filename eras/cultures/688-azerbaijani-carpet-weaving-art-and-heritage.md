# Azerbaijani Carpet Weaving: Art and Heritage

Azerbaijani carpet weaving is far more than a domestic craft; it is a profound visual language that encodes the history, spirituality, and geography of the South Caucasus. For centuries, these textiles have served as portable archives, reflecting the synthesis of nomadic traditions, sedentary urban culture, and the cross-cultural exchanges occurring along the ancient Silk Road.

### Overview
The art of Azerbaijani carpets is characterized by a sophisticated interplay of geometry and nature. These carpets are traditionally divided into several regional schools—most notably Guba, Shirvan, Baku, and Karabakh—each possessing distinct stylistic markers. While Guba carpets are known for their intricate, small-scale patterns, Karabakh carpets often feature larger, floral compositions reflecting the lush landscapes of the region.

The symbolism embedded in the weaves is deeply rooted in ancient beliefs. Common motifs include the *buta* (a stylized flame or almond shape representing eternity and fire), geometric stars, and stylized animals. These elements were not merely decorative; they often acted as talismans or narratives describing the weaver's social status, family lineage, and hopes for the future. In 2010, this enduring legacy was formally recognized by UNESCO, which inscribed Azerbaijani carpet weaving on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity.

### Timeline of Evolution

| Era | Significance |

### Key Facts
*   **Materials:** Traditional carpets are crafted from high-quality wool, though silk is frequently used in urban workshops (particularly in Baku) to create high-luster, intricate pieces.
*   **Natural Pigments:** Historically, colors were derived from nature: madder root for red, indigo for blue, and pomegranate peel or walnut shells for browns and yellows.
*   **The Loom:** The vertical loom is the primary tool, allowing the weaver to maintain tension and precision over large-scale compositions.
*   **Regional Diversity:** The "Baku" style is often associated with luxury and complexity, whereas "Shirvan" carpets are prized for their structural density and refined symmetry.

### Did You Know?
1. **The Flame Symbolism:** The prevalence of fire-like motifs in Azerbaijani carpets is a nod to the region's ancient identity as the "Land of Fire," referencing both natural gas vents and Zoroastrian traditions.
2. **A Silent Language:** In antiquity, certain carpet patterns could communicate a woman's marital status or her region of origin to those who knew how to "read" the weave.
3. **Global Reach:** Azerbaijani carpets were highly coveted in the royal courts of Persia and the Ottoman Empire, often serving as diplomatic gifts between monarchs.
