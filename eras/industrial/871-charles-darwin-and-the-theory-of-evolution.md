# Charles Darwin and the Architecture of Life

### Overview
The mid-19th century was an era of seismic shifts. While the Industrial Revolution was mechanically transforming the landscape of Britain—replacing cottages with factories and horses with steam engines—Charles Darwin was orchestrating a similar revolution in the realm of thought. In 1859, with the publication of *On the Origin of Species*, Darwin challenged the static view of nature, proposing that life was not a series of immutable creations, but a fluid, evolving process.

Darwin’s theory of evolution by natural selection suggested that individuals with traits better suited to their environment were more likely to survive and reproduce. Over vast stretches of geological time, these advantageous traits accumulated, leading to the emergence of new species. This conceptual leap mirrored the industrial spirit of the age: it was a study of efficiency, adaptation, and systemic change. However, while steam power was welcomed for its utility, Darwin’s theories sparked intense theological and social conflict, as they displaced humanity from a pedestal of special creation to a branch on the great tree of life.

### Key Facts
*   **Natural Selection:** The primary mechanism of evolution. Darwin observed that nature "selects" the fittest organisms based on their ability to survive environmental pressures.
*   **Common Descent:** Darwin proposed that all living organisms share a common ancestor, a concept known as "descent with modification."
*   **The Beagle's Influence:** The HMS Beagle was not a biological expedition but a surveying mission. Darwin served as a naturalist and gentleman companion to Captain Robert FitzRoy.
*   **Scientific Rigor:** Darwin did not publish his findings immediately. He spent over twenty years gathering evidence from pigeon breeding, botany, and geology to ensure his theory could withstand scrutiny.

### Did You Know?
1. **The "Wallace" Scare:** Darwin had been working on his theory in secret for nearly two decades until Alfred Russel Wallace sent him a manuscript outlining nearly identical ideas, prompting Darwin to finally publish.
2. **A Love for Earthworms:** In his later years, Darwin became obsessed with earthworms, spending years studying how their constant churning of soil shaped the physical landscape.
3. **The "Monkey" Myth:** Contrary to popular caricatures, Darwin never claimed that humans "evolved from monkeys." Instead, he argued that humans and modern apes share a common ancestor.
