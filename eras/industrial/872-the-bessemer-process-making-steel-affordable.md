# The Bessemer Process: Making Steel Affordable

## Overview
For centuries, steel was a luxury material. While stronger and more flexible than cast iron, it was prohibitively expensive to produce because it required a laborious, slow process of removing impurities from iron. Steel was reserved for high-end cutlery, swords, and specialized tools; it was simply too costly for large-scale infrastructure.

This changed in the mid-19th century with the introduction of the **Bessemer process**, patented by Sir Henry Bessemer in 1856. Bessemer’s breakthrough was the realization that impurities—specifically carbon, silicon, and manganese—could be removed from molten pig iron by blowing air through it. This triggered a chemical reaction called oxidation, which burned off the impurities and released immense heat, keeping the metal molten without the need for extra fuel.

By reducing the time required to turn iron into steel from several days to roughly 20 minutes, the Bessemer process crashed the price of steel. This democratization of the metal fueled the "Second Industrial Revolution," enabling the construction of massive bridges, skyscrapers, and thousands of miles of durable railway tracks that could withstand the weight of heavier, faster locomotives.

## Key Facts
*   **The Bessemer Converter:** The heart of the process was a large, pear-shaped vessel that could tilt. Air was blasted through the bottom of the molten iron, creating a spectacular "boil" of sparks and flames.
*   **Cost Reduction:** The cost of steel dropped precipitously, falling from roughly £40 per ton to nearly £7 per ton in some regions, making it commercially viable for general construction.
*   **Urbanization:** Without affordable steel, the modern skyline would not exist. Steel beams allowed buildings to grow vertically, leading to the birth of the skyscraper in cities like Chicago and New York.
*   **The Phosphorus Problem:** The original process failed with iron ores containing high phosphorus. This was later solved by the "Basic Bessemer" process (the Gilchrist-Thomas process), which used a limestone lining in the converter.

## Did You Know?
1. **A Violent Reaction:** The process was so energetic that the converter would erupt in a towering pillar of fire, making a steel mill one of the most visually dramatic sites of the industrial era.
2. **Railway Revolution:** Before Bessemer, iron rails had to be replaced every few years due to wear. Steel rails lasted ten times longer, drastically lowering maintenance costs for the expanding rail networks.
3. **The "Steel King":** Andrew Carnegie utilized the Bessemer process to build his empire, implementing the technology on a massive scale to dominate the American steel industry.
