# The Electric Nerve: The Telegraph and the Shrinking World

## Overview
During the Industrial Revolution, the pace of human life was dictated by the speed of a galloping horse or a sailing ship. Information was physical; to send a message from London to New York meant enduring a weeks-long voyage across the Atlantic. The advent of the telegraph in the mid-19th century fundamentally shattered this limitation, decoupling communication from transportation for the first time in human history.

By utilizing electrical pulses sent over copper wires, the telegraph transformed the "weeks" of transit into "seconds" of transmission. This innovation did more than just speed up mail; it revolutionized global diplomacy, synchronized international financial markets, and laid the groundwork for the modern internet. The culmination of this era was the laying of the Transatlantic Cable, a feat of Victorian engineering that effectively turned the world into a single, interconnected community.

## Key Facts

### The Language of Dots and Dashes
While early telegraphs used needles to point to letters, Samuel Morse’s "Morse Code" became the global standard. By representing letters as sequences of short (dots) and long (dots) pulses, complex language could be transmitted through a simple on-off switch.

### Economic Acceleration
The telegraph birthed the modern stock market. Before its arrival, price discrepancies between cities were common because news of a price change took days to travel. With the telegraph, "arbitrage" decreased, and markets became synchronized in real-time, fueling the rapid growth of industrial capitalism.

### Geopolitical Control
For empires, the telegraph was a tool of administration. The British Empire, in particular, used the "All Red Line" (a network of cables touching only British soil) to maintain tight control over distant colonies, allowing London to issue orders to India or Canada almost instantaneously.

## Did You Know?

*   **The "Victorian Internet":** Historians often call the telegraph the "Victorian Internet" because it created the first global social network, allowing people to share news and personal messages across borders instantly.
*   **Deep Sea Struggle:** The first transatlantic cables were coated in **gutta-percha**, a natural latex from Malaysian trees, which provided the only effective insulation against the crushing pressure and saltwater of the ocean floor.
*   **The Death of the Courier:** Before the telegraph, "express riders" were the fastest way to send news. The telegraph rendered these professions obsolete almost overnight, changing the labor landscape of the 19th century.
