# Victorian London: Life in the Industrial Capital

## Overview
During the 19th century, London underwent a metamorphosis, evolving from a sprawling Georgian town into the undisputed heartbeat of the British Empire and the industrial world. This era was defined by a jarring dichotomy: unparalleled wealth and global influence existing side-by-side with crushing poverty and environmental decay.

As the Industrial Revolution accelerated, London became a magnet for laborers from the countryside and immigrants from across the globe. The city grew at a rate that outpaced its infrastructure, leading to the creation of notorious slums and "rookeries." While the West End showcased the opulence of the rising middle class and the aristocracy, the East End became a symbol of industrial grit and social desperation. The air, thick with "pea-souper" fogs—a toxic blend of coal smoke and river mist—became a permanent fixture of the urban landscape. Yet, this period was also one of brilliant innovation, witnessing the birth of the underground railway, the professionalization of policing, and the dawn of modern public health.

## Key Facts

*   **The Great Stink:** By the mid-1850s, the Thames was essentially an open sewer. In the summer of 1858, the smell became so unbearable that Parliament had to drape curtains soaked in chloride of lime to mask the stench, eventually leading to Joseph Bazalgette’s revolutionary sewer network.
*   **Social Stratification:** The city was strictly divided by class. The "Gentry" lived in townhouses with servants, while the "working poor" often lived in single-room tenements, sharing a single water pump with dozens of neighbors.
*   **Economic Hub:** London served as the financial center of the world. The Port of London was the busiest in existence, importing tea, silk, and spices from the colonies to fuel the domestic economy.
*   **Public Health Crisis:** Overcrowding and lack of sanitation led to frequent outbreaks of cholera and typhus. This spurred the "Sanitary Movement," which fundamentally changed how cities managed waste and water.

## Did You Know?

1. **Mudlarks and Pure Finders:** The poorest residents, including children, survived by "mudlarking"—scavenging the banks of the Thames for scrap metal or coal—or by collecting dog dung ("pure") to sell to tanneries.
2. **The First Police Force:** The Metropolitan Police Service was established in 1829 by Sir Robert Peel. The officers were nicknamed "Bobbies" after him.
3. **The Fog was Deadly:** The famous Victorian fogs weren't just weather; they were "smogs" (smoke + fog). The sulfur dioxide from coal fires reacted with water droplets, creating a mild sulfuric acid that damaged lungs and obscured vision.
