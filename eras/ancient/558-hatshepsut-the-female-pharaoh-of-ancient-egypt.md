# Hatshepsut: The Female Pharaoh of Ancient Egypt

## Overview
In the annals of ancient Egyptian history, few figures are as provocative or successful as Hatshepsut. Ruling during the 18th Dynasty of the New Kingdom, Hatshepsut began her tenure as a regent for her young stepson, Thutmose III. However, in an unprecedented move, she transitioned from regent to pharaoh, claiming the full titles and powers of the throne.

Unlike many of her successors who sought glory through military conquest, Hatshepsut’s reign was defined by economic prosperity, architectural ambition, and the restoration of trade networks. She skillfully navigated the patriarchal constraints of Egyptian society by aligning herself with the divine, claiming that the god Amun-Ra was her true father. By blending feminine grace with the traditional iconography of a king—including the wearing of the ceremonial false beard—she established a period of stability and wealth that cemented her legacy as one of Egypt's most effective rulers.

## Timeline of Power

| Period | Event | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **c. 1479 BCE** | Death of Thutmose II | Hatshepsut becomes regent for the infant Thutmose III. |
| **c. 1473 BCE** | Coronation as Pharaoh | She officially assumes the title of King, ruling as a co-regent. |
| **c. 1460 BCE** | Expedition to Punt | A massive trade mission brings incense, gold, and ebony to Egypt. |
| **c. 1458 BCE** | Death of Hatshepsut | Thutmose III begins his sole reign. |
| **Post-Reign** | Damnatio Memoriae | Efforts are made to erase her name and image from monuments. |

## Key Facts

*   **The Divine Right:** To justify her rule, Hatshepsut promoted the myth of her "divine birth," claiming Amun-Ra had visited her mother in the guise of her father to conceive her.
*   **Architectural Legacy:** Her most famous achievement is the *Djeser-Djeseru* (the "Holy of Holies"), her mortuary temple at Deir el-Bahari. This masterpiece of ancient architecture is carved directly into the limestone cliffs of the Theban hills.
*   **Economic Expansion:** She shifted the national focus from warfare to commerce. Her expedition to the Land of Punt (likely modern-day Somalia or Eritrea) was a logistical triumph that brought exotic luxuries and frankincense trees into Egypt.
*   **The Great Erasure:** Years after her death, Thutmose III (or his successor) ordered the systematic destruction of her images and the chiseling away of her name from obelisks. Historians believe this was not out of hatred, but to ensure the legitimacy of the male line of succession.

## Did You Know?

1. **The False Beard:** Hatshepsut was often depicted in statues wearing the traditional pharaonic false beard to signal her authority and adherence to the "office" of the king.
2. **The Obelisk Record:** She commissioned two massive obelisks at the Temple of Karnak; one remains the tallest surviving ancient obelisk in Egypt.
3. **Rediscovered Identity:** For centuries, her existence was forgotten until 19th-century archaeologists decoded the hieroglyphs, revealing that a woman had once ruled Egypt as a king.
