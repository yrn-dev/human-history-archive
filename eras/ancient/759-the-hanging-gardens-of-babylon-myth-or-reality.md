# The Hanging Gardens of Babylon: Myth or Reality?

For centuries, the Hanging Gardens of Babylon have captivated the human imagination as one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. Described as a lush, ascending series of tiered gardens filled with exotic flora, they represented a triumph of engineering and a romantic gesture of imperial love. However, for modern historians and archaeologists, the Gardens present a profound mystery: they are the only "Wonder" whose location has never been definitively established.

### Overview
According to classical accounts—most notably those of Berossus and Diodorus Siculus—the gardens were commissioned by King Nebuchadnezzar II (r. 605–562 BCE) for his wife, Amytis of Media. Amytis, hailing from the green mountains of Persia, reportedly suffered from homesickness in the flat, arid landscape of Mesopotamia. To comfort her, Nebuchadnezzar allegedly constructed a massive artificial mountain of stone and brick, irrigated by a complex system of pumps and screws that lifted water from the Euphrates River.

The central tension in historical discourse is the total absence of Babylonian records mentioning the gardens. While Nebuchadnezzar’s building projects are meticulously documented in cuneiform tablets, the gardens are conspicuously missing. This has led many historians to believe the gardens were either a poetic exaggeration by Greek writers or a complete myth.

### Timeline of the Legend

| Era | Event | Significance |

### Key Facts
*   **Engineering Marvel:** If they existed, the gardens required advanced waterproofing (using lead and bitumen) to prevent the water from rotting the brick structures beneath the soil.
*   **The Nineveh Alternative:** Recent scholarship suggests the gardens were actually built by the Assyrian King Sennacherib in Nineveh, not Nebuchadnezzar in Babylon. This theory is supported by Assyrian reliefs showing lush gardens and sophisticated aqueducts.
*   **Botanical Diversity:** Descriptions suggest the gardens featured a variety of trees and shrubs imported from across the Near East, creating a microclimate that defied the desert heat.

### Did You Know?
1. **Missing Evidence:** Despite decades of excavation, no Babylonian text mentions the gardens; they are known exclusively through the writings of later Greek and Roman historians.
2. **Ancient Pumps:** Some theorists believe the gardens utilized an early version of the "Archimedes screw" to move water vertically, predating the mathematician himself.
3. **Symbolism:** Regardless of their physical existence, the gardens served as a powerful symbol of the "Paradise" garden (from the Old Persian *pairidaeza*), influencing landscape architecture for millennia.
